The Mysterious Dancing Plague of 1518

Wed 04 October 2023
By Andrew Rosario
Category: Blog

In the summer of 1518, a peculiar and inexplicable phenomenon swept through the streets of Strasbourg, a city in what is now France. It was the Dancing Plague of 1518, a bizarre event that saw hundreds of people spontaneously start dancing and unable to stop, sometimes for days on end. This strange episode remains one of history's most mysterious and enigmatic occurrences.

The bizarre outbreak began with a woman named Frau Troffea. On a seemingly ordinary day in July, she stepped into the streets of Strasbourg and began to dance. She danced with an unrelenting fervor, and what was even more unusual was that she showed no signs of joy or pleasure. Her compulsion to dance was involuntary, and she couldn't control it.

Days turned into weeks, and Frau Troffea's solo dance act garnered the attention of curious onlookers. Instead of subsiding, her strange dance fever seemed to spread like a contagion. Within a month, dozens of people joined her in this seemingly never-ending dance, and the number continued to grow.

Authorities, baffled and concerned about the physical and psychological toll this was taking on the dancers, tried to intervene. They attempted to halt the dancing by providing musicians, hoping that playing music would help the dancers find some respite. They even set up a stage and hired more musicians, but these efforts had an unintended consequence - more people joined in the frenzied dance.

As the weeks passed, the Dancing Plague reached its peak, with hundreds of people caught in the grip of this inexplicable compulsion. Many suffered from exhaustion, dehydration, and even heart attacks, but they couldn't stop dancing.

Medical experts and theologians of the time struggled to explain this bizarre event. Some attributed it to "hot blood," while others believed it was a divine punishment for sinning. No one had a definitive answer, and the plague continued to baffle those who witnessed it.

Eventually, the Dancing Plague of 1518 began to wane, leaving behind a trail of exhausted and traumatized survivors. It's estimated that several people died during the episode, their bodies simply giving out from sheer exhaustion.

To this day, historians and scientists have proposed various theories to explain the Dancing Plague, ranging from mass hysteria and psychogenic illness to food poisoning caused by a fungus that led to hallucinations and convulsions. However, the exact cause remains a mystery.

The Dancing Plague of 1518 serves as a haunting reminder of the complexity of the human mind and the strange ways in which our bodies can react to stress and societal pressures. It remains a historical enigma, a testament to the limits of our understanding, and a curious fact that continues to capture the imagination of those who encounter it.
